About Patnanungan

An island municipality cradled in the Polillo Islands group of Quezon Province — where nature, community, and heritage intertwine.

History  &Origins

Patnanungan is a fifth-class island municipality in the province of Quezon, Philippines, belonging to the Polillo Islands group situated in the Philippine Sea.

The municipality's name is believed to derive from the Tagalog word "tunong," referring to a type of local plant abundant in the area. Over generations, the fishing and farming communities that settled on the island developed a distinct island culture shaped by the rhythms of the sea and land.

Before its formal establishment as a municipality, Patnanungan was part of Polillo. It was eventually recognized as its own political unit, allowing its people to govern their unique island community with greater autonomy.

The island's relative isolation — accessible only by sea — has preserved much of its natural landscape and traditional way of life, making it one of the less-commercialized destinations in Quezon Province.

Geography & Natural Resources

Patnanungan is part of the Polillo Island Group, surrounded by the Philippine Sea to the east and the Lamon Bay to the west. The island features a diverse landscape of white-sand beaches, rocky outcroppings, dense secondary forests, and fertile agricultural areas.

The surrounding waters are ecologically significant, home to diverse coral reef ecosystems that support a rich variety of marine life including fish, sea turtles, and invertebrates. These marine resources are the backbone of the community's fishing-based economy.

The island's interior features lowland forests and agricultural areas where residents cultivate root crops, vegetables, and coconuts. The combination of rich marine and terrestrial resources makes Patnanungan one of the more self-sufficient island municipalities in Quezon.

The local community is primarily engaged in fishing, small-scale farming, and increasingly, tourism-related activities as awareness of the island's natural beauty continues to grow among travelers seeking off-the-beaten-path Philippine destinations.

Community & Livelihood

The people of Patnanungan are primarily fishermen and farmers — communities whose lives follow the natural rhythms of tide and harvest. Fishing is not merely an economic activity here; it is a cultural practice passed down through generations, with each family having its own knowledge of local fishing grounds, seasonal patterns, and traditional techniques.

Small-scale farming supports the community with crops like camote, cassava, gabi, and various leafy vegetables. Coconut farming provides both food and a small income stream through copra production.

Women in the community often run sari-sari stores, manage the household finances, and produce handicrafts and food products for local sale. This gender-complementary economic model has helped maintain family stability even in a resource-limited island setting.

Tourism has begun to supplement the local economy, with some families converting spare rooms to guesthouses or offering bangka services and guided island tours to visitors. This gradual shift is being approached cautiously by the community, with an emphasis on preserving the island's natural and cultural heritage.
